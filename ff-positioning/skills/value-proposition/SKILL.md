# Build Your Value Proposition

Articulate exactly why your product matters - to a specific person, for a specific reason. This skill uses a 6-part JTBD framework that forces clarity on who you serve, what they're struggling with, and why your solution is better than what they use today.

## Context

You are helping a founder build a value proposition for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides positioning work, customer research, competitor analysis, or prior messaging, read those first.

## The Framework

This uses a 6-part JTBD value proposition template. The Jobs to Be Done framework was developed by Clayton Christensen, detailed in "Competing Against Luck" (2016). The three-job structure (functional, emotional, social) comes from that work. See also Tony Ulwick, "Jobs to Be Done: Theory to Practice". The framework focuses on the PROGRESS a customer is trying to make - not their demographics, not their feature wishlist, but the outcome they're hiring a product to achieve.

**Why JTBD over other frameworks:**
- The Value Proposition Canvas by Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur ("Value Proposition Design", 2014) is useful but complex (splitting jobs, pains, gains across two sides creates confusion about where things go). This 6-part template produces the same insight with less friction.
- Feature-benefit lists miss the "before and after" transformation that makes value propositions compelling.
- Generic value prop templates ("We help X do Y") are too shallow. The 6-part structure forces you to understand the full customer journey.

### Step 1: Define Each Component

Work through all 6 parts. Each builds on the previous one.

**Part 1: WHO**
Who specifically is this value proposition for?

Not "small businesses" or "developers" - a specific segment with specific characteristics:
- What role or situation are they in?
- What's their skill level with this type of product?
- What constraints do they operate under? (budget, time, team size, technical ability)
- What's their context? (industry, company size, stage)

Example: "Freelance web designers working solo, managing 3-8 client projects simultaneously, technically skilled but don't want to spend time on admin."

**Part 2: WHY (The Job to Be Done)**
What problem are they trying to solve? What progress are they trying to make?

Frame this as a "job" they need done:
- **Functional job**: The practical task (e.g., "send professional invoices quickly")
- **Emotional job**: How they want to feel (e.g., "feel like a legitimate business, not a freelancer winging it")
- **Social job**: How they want to be perceived (e.g., "look professional to clients")

Most products succeed by addressing all three jobs, not just the functional one.

**Part 3: WHAT BEFORE (Current State)**
What is the customer's situation RIGHT NOW, before using your product?

- What are they currently using? (competitor, manual process, nothing)
- What frustrates them about their current approach?
- What's broken, slow, expensive, or painful?
- What workarounds have they cobbled together?

This section creates the "before" picture that makes the "after" picture compelling.

**Part 4: HOW (Your Solution)**
How does your product solve their job to be done?

- What specific capabilities address their frustrations?
- What's the experience like? (simple, fast, automated, guided)
- What makes your approach different from the alternatives in Part 3?

Be specific. "AI-powered" is meaningless. "Automatically generates client-ready summaries from raw meeting notes in under 60 seconds" is specific.

**Part 5: WHAT AFTER (Transformed State)**
What does the customer's life/work look like AFTER using your product?

- What becomes possible that wasn't before?
- How much time/money/effort do they save?
- How do they feel? (confident, in control, professional, relieved)
- What new outcomes do they achieve?

The gap between "What Before" and "What After" is your value. The bigger the gap, the stronger the proposition.

**Part 6: ALTERNATIVES (Why You, Not Them?)**
What other options could the customer choose, and why should they choose you?

If you've already completed the positioning-canvas skill, import competitive alternatives from there rather than repeating the analysis.

- Name 2-3 specific alternatives (products, manual processes, hiring someone)
- For each: what do they do well, and where do they fall short?
- What is your decisive advantage over each?

This forces honest competitive positioning. If you can't articulate why you're better than a specific alternative for your specific audience, your value proposition is weak.

### Step 2: Write the Value Proposition Statement

Condense the 6 parts into a clear statement:

**[Product name] helps [WHO] [achieve WHAT AFTER] by [HOW]. Unlike [top ALTERNATIVE], [product name] [key differentiator that addresses WHY and WHAT BEFORE pain].**

### Step 3: Create the Value Curve (Blue Ocean)

Map your product against 2-3 alternatives on the factors your customer cares about.

Derive the value curve factors from the "What Before" frustrations identified in Part 3. Each frustration suggests a factor. If the user complains about "too complicated" - add "Ease of use" as a factor. If they complain about "too expensive" - add "Price" as a factor.

| Factor | Your Product | Alternative 1 | Alternative 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Factor 1: e.g., Ease of use] | [High/Med/Low] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] |
| [Factor 2: e.g., Price] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] |
| [Factor 3: e.g., Speed to value] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] |
| [Factor 4: e.g., Customisation] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] |
| [Factor 5: e.g., Support quality] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] | [H/M/L] |

The value curve shows WHERE you differentiate. If your curve looks the same as competitors, you don't have a value proposition - you have a clone.

```
## Value Proposition: [Product Name]

**Date**: [today]

### 1. WHO
[Detailed description of target customer]

### 2. WHY (Job to Be Done)
- **Functional job**: [practical task]
- **Emotional job**: [how they want to feel]
- **Social job**: [how they want to be perceived]

### 3. WHAT BEFORE (Current State)
- **Current solution**: [what they use today]
- **Key frustrations**: [list]
- **Workarounds**: [what they've cobbled together]

### 4. HOW (Your Solution)
- [Capability 1 and what it does for them]
- [Capability 2 and what it does for them]
- [Capability 3 and what it does for them]

### 5. WHAT AFTER (Transformed State)
- [Outcome 1: state as "[metric] [reduced/increased/eliminated] by [amount or %] within [timeframe]". If you cannot quantify, describe the specific before/after behavioural change.]
- [Outcome 2]
- [Emotional outcome: how they feel]

### 6. ALTERNATIVES
| Alternative | Strengths | Weaknesses | Our Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| [name] | [what they do well] | [where they fall short] | [why we win] |
| [name] | [strengths] | [weaknesses] | [our advantage] |

### Value Proposition Statement
[Product name] helps [WHO] [achieve WHAT AFTER] by [HOW]. Unlike [top ALTERNATIVE], [product name] [key differentiator].

### Value Curve

|---|---|---|---|
| [factor] | [rating] | [rating] | [rating] |
```

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing the value prop for yourself, not your customer.** "We use cutting-edge AI" is about you. "You get meeting notes in 30 seconds instead of 30 minutes" is about them.
- **Skipping the emotional and social jobs.** Products win on emotion as much as function. A freelancer doesn't just want invoicing - they want to feel like a real business.
- **Vague "What After."** "Save time" is vague. "Save 3 hours per week on meeting admin" is specific and compelling.
- **Not naming alternatives.** If you don't know what you're competing against, your positioning is built on sand.
- **One value prop for all audiences.** Different segments have different jobs to be done. Create separate value propositions for each key segment.

## Notes

- This skill works best after positioning-canvas (which defines your market position) and before elevator-pitch (which turns the value prop into a concise message)
- Value propositions should be tested with real customers. Show it to 5 people in your target segment and ask: "Does this describe a problem you have? Would this solution interest you?"
- Revisit your value proposition whenever you learn something new about your customers
