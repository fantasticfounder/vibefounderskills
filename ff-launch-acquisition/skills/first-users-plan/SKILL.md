# Get Your First 100 Users

You built the app. Now you need a real plan to find users - not just "post on Reddit." This skill combines market strategy with tactical execution to create an acquisition plan that actually works.

## Context

You are helping a founder get their first users for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides details about their app, target audience, competitors, or any prior marketing, read those first.

## Why This Matters

Most solo founders skip strategy and go straight to tactics: post on Reddit, launch on ProductHunt, DM people on Twitter. Tactics without strategy is random action. You need to know WHO you're targeting, WHERE they are, and WHY they'd care - before you decide HOW to reach them.

## Instructions

Read CONVENTIONS.md for web search policy, context threading, and scoring conventions. These apply throughout this skill.

### Step 1: Understand the Product and Market

Ask the user (if not already clear):
- What does your app do? (one sentence)
- Who is it for? (specific audience - job title, industry, situation)
- What problem does it solve? What are people doing TODAY without your app?
- Is it live? Can people sign up right now?
- What's your pricing model? (free, freemium, paid, lifetime deal)
- Do you have ANY users yet? How did they find you?
- Who are the closest competitors or alternatives?
- Do you have any existing analytics or sign-up source data? Real data overrides any scoring framework.

### Step 2: Size the Opportunity (Quick TAM/SAM/SOM)

Even as a solo founder, you need to know your market size. Not for a pitch deck - for knowing whether 100 users is realistic and where to find them.

**TAM (Total Addressable Market)**: Everyone who COULD use your product type.
- How many people/companies have the problem your app solves?
- Use web search, industry reports, or proxy data (subreddit sizes, competitor user counts, job listing volumes)
- If the user has not provided market size data, use web search. Search: '[audience] market size', '[product type] industry report'. Use subreddit subscriber counts and competitor user counts as proxies. Always state when you're estimating vs using real data.

**SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)**: The portion you can actually reach.
- Filter by geography, language, tech stack, willingness to use your type of product
- For most indie products, SAM = people who actively search for solutions online

**SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)**: What you can realistically capture in 6-12 months.
- With your current resources (solo founder, limited budget), how many users can you realistically acquire and serve?
- For most indie products starting out, SOM is 100-1,000 users in Year 1

Help the user estimate these. Rough numbers are fine - the point is sanity-checking whether 100 users is 0.01% of the market (easy to find) or 50% (probably unrealistic).

### Step 3: Define Your Beachhead Segment

Don't target "everyone who could use this." Pick ONE specific segment to start with - the group most likely to adopt first. This is your beachhead.

Evaluate potential segments against four criteria:

| Criteria | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| **Pain intensity** | How urgent is this problem for them? Daily frustration? Costing them money? |
| **Reachability** | Can you actually reach them? Are they in communities you can access? Do they search for solutions? |
| **Willingness to pay** | Will they pay for a solution, or do they expect free? Is the ROI clear to them? |
| **Referral potential** | Will they tell others? Are they connected to adjacent segments you can expand into? |

Score 2-3 candidate segments and pick the one with the highest combined score.

Example:
- App: AI meeting notes tool
- Segment A: Enterprise sales teams (high pain, hard to reach, will pay, moderate referral)
- Segment B: Freelance consultants (moderate pain, easy to reach on Twitter/Reddit, price-sensitive, high referral in communities)
- Segment C: Student study groups (low pain, easy to reach, won't pay, low referral to paying users)
- **Beachhead**: Segment B - freelance consultants. Reachable, active in communities, will convert to paid if value is clear.

### Step 4: Map Channel-Market Fit

Now match your beachhead segment to the channels where they actually discover products. This is channel-market fit - not "which channels are trendy" but "where does YOUR segment find new tools."

For each candidate channel, assess:

| Factor | Question |
|---|---|
| **Audience presence** | Is your beachhead segment active here? |
| **Discovery behaviour** | Do they discover products here, or just socialise? |
| **Content format fit** | Does your product demo well in this format? (visual = TikTok/YouTube, technical = Reddit/HN, B2B = LinkedIn/email) |
| **Competition** | Are competitors already dominating this channel? |
| **Effort vs. reach** | How much effort for how many eyeballs? |

Score channels 1-5 on each factor and rank them. Pick your top 3.

Common channel-segment matches:

| Segment Type | High-Fit Channels |
|---|---|
| Developers / technical founders | Reddit, Hacker News, GitHub, Dev.to, Twitter/X |
| Freelancers / solopreneurs | Twitter/X, IndieHackers, Reddit, niche communities |
| Small business owners | Google Search (SEO), Facebook Groups, LinkedIn, directories |
| Designers / creatives | Twitter/X, Dribbble, ProductHunt, TikTok |
| B2B / enterprise users | LinkedIn, cold email, partnerships, content marketing |
| Consumer / general audience | TikTok, Instagram, SEO, YouTube, app stores |

### Step 5: Design the Acquisition Funnel

For each channel, define how a stranger becomes a user:

**Awareness** -> **Interest** -> **Activation**

- **Awareness**: How do they first hear about you? (post, ad, search result, referral)
- **Interest**: What makes them click? (headline, demo, problem statement)
- **Activation**: What makes them sign up and USE the product? (landing page, onboarding, free tier)

If any step in the funnel is broken, users leak out. A great Reddit post that links to a confusing landing page = wasted effort.

### Step 6: Create the Execution Plan

Build a 4-week plan with specific, trackable actions.

**Per-channel action guidance** - use this when filling in channel-specific actions in the plan:

- **For Reddit**: Join 3 relevant subreddits, comment on 5+ posts before making your own, draft a 'Show Your Project' or story post, NOT a pitch
- **For Twitter/X**: Post daily about the problem you solve, engage with 10 accounts in your niche, share a build-in-public thread
- **For HN**: Prepare a Show HN post, time for 9am-12pm EST weekdays, focus on the technical or novel aspect
- **For cold outreach**: Send 5-10 personalised messages per day using cold-outreach-draft templates
- **For SEO**: Publish 1 blog post targeting your top BOFU keyword, ensure it's indexed

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping segmentation.** "My app is for everyone" means your marketing speaks to nobody. Pick a beachhead.
- **Picking channels you LIKE instead of channels your audience USES.** You might love Twitter, but if your audience is accountants, they're on LinkedIn and Google.
- **Tactics without strategy.** "Post on Reddit" is a tactic. "Target freelance consultants who search for meeting note tools, reach them through r/freelance and r/consulting where they discuss productivity pain points" is a strategy.
- **No funnel thinking.** You got 1,000 views on a post but 2 sign-ups? The problem isn't awareness - it's your landing page, pricing, or value proposition.
- **Giving up after a week.** First users take 2-4 weeks of consistent effort, not one weekend.

## Notes

- The first 10 users are the hardest. After 10, you can ask for referrals and feedback.
- Quality > quantity for first users. 10 users who give feedback are worth more than 100 ghost sign-ups.
- If you haven't validated demand yet, run the validate-idea skill first. Acquisition without demand = pushing a boulder uphill.
- This plan assumes limited budget. If you have budget for paid acquisition, it can accelerate channels - but organic-first is smarter until you know what converts.
