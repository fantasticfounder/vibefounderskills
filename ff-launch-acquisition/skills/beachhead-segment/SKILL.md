# Find Your Beachhead Segment

You can't sell to everyone at once. This skill finds the ONE specific group to target first - the group most likely to adopt, pay, and refer others. Once you dominate this beachhead, you expand from there.

## Context

You are helping a founder identify their beachhead market segment for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides details about their product, existing users, competitors, or market research, read those first.

## The Framework

This is based on Geoffrey Moore's beachhead strategy from "Crossing the Chasm" - the idea that new products should dominate a tiny, specific market segment before expanding. Combined with additional evaluation criteria from competitive strategy and market entry frameworks.

Why it matters: most products fail not because they're bad, but because they try to serve too many people at once. The founder spreads resources thin, the messaging is generic, and nobody feels like "this was made for me."

A beachhead segment solves this. It gives you:
- **Focus**: All marketing speaks directly to one group
- **Speed to feedback**: Faster learning from a concentrated user base
- **Word of mouth**: Tight communities spread recommendations faster
- **Credibility**: "We're the [product type] for [specific segment]" is more compelling than "We're for everyone"

### Step 1: List Candidate Segments

Help the user brainstorm 4-8 potential segments. Segments can be sliced by:

| Dimension | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Industry / vertical** | SaaS founders, real estate agents, fitness coaches, ecommerce sellers |
| **Company size** | Solo founders, small teams (2-10), mid-market (50-500), enterprise |
| **Role / job title** | Freelance designers, startup CTOs, marketing managers, content creators |
| **Situation / trigger** | Just launched a product, just raised funding, just hired first employee |
| **Geography** | US market, UK market, LATAM, specific cities |
| **Tech stack / platform** | Shopify store owners, WordPress users, React developers, no-code builders |
| **Behaviour** | Active on Reddit, heavy Slack users, conference attendees, newsletter subscribers |

Good segments are specific enough that you could find 100 people who match in under an hour.

### Step 2: Evaluate Against 6 Criteria

Score each candidate segment 1-5 on these criteria:

**1. Pain Intensity (How urgently do they need this?)**
- 5: Daily frustration, actively searching for a solution, current workarounds are painful
- 3: Aware of the problem, would use a solution if presented
- 1: Vaguely aware of the problem, not motivated to solve it

**2. Willingness to Pay (Will they spend money on this?)**
- 5: Clear ROI, budget exists, precedent for paying for this type of solution
- 3: Would consider paying if value is proven, price-sensitive but not zero-budget
- 1: Expect free solutions, no budget, culture of not paying for tools

**3. Reachability (Can you actually get in front of them?)**
- 5: Active in specific communities you can access, searchable, respond to outreach
- 3: Online presence but scattered, requires multiple channels to reach
- 1: Hard to find, don't congregate online, gatekept by intermediaries

**4. Market Size (Is the segment big enough to sustain a business?)**
- 5: Thousands of potential users in this segment alone
- 3: Hundreds of potential users, enough to validate and learn
- 1: Fewer than 50 potential users total - too small to build a business on

**5. Referral Potential (Will they tell others?)**
- 5: Active in communities, connected to adjacent segments, culture of sharing tools
- 3: Will recommend if asked, some community participation
- 1: Isolated, don't share tools, weak community ties

**6. Competitive Landscape (Can you win this segment?)**

The 6th criterion (competitive landscape) draws from Porter's Five Forces analysis of competitive intensity (Porter, 'Competitive Strategy', 1980).

- 5: No established player targeting this specific segment, wide-open opportunity
- 3: Some competition but room to differentiate on features, price, or experience
- 1: Dominant player already owns this segment, high switching costs

### Step 3: Apply Strategic Lenses

Beyond the scoring, apply these strategic filters:

**Bowling Pin Strategy (Moore)**: Your beachhead should be the first "pin" - when it falls, it naturally knocks over adjacent segments. A segment with no expansion path is a dead end.

Ask: "After we win THIS segment, which segment falls next? And after that?"

**Minimum Viable Segment**: The smallest segment that can sustain your business. Too big = you can't focus. Too small = you can't sustain revenue. For most indie SaaS, the sweet spot is 500-5,000 potential users.

**Founder-Market Fit**: Do you have unique insight, access, or credibility with this segment? A founder who IS the target customer has an unfair advantage in understanding their needs and earning trust.

### Step 4: Map Expansion Paths

For the top 2-3 segments, map out where you go AFTER the beachhead:

```
Beachhead Segment -> Adjacent Segment 1 -> Adjacent Segment 2 -> Broader Market
```

The best beachhead has clear, logical expansion paths. Example:
- Beachhead: Freelance web designers
- Adjacent 1: Small design agencies (2-5 people)
- Adjacent 2: Marketing agencies needing design tools
- Broader: Any small business creating visual content

### Step 5: Make the Recommendation

Pick one beachhead and justify it clearly.

## Common Mistakes

- **"Everyone" is not a segment.** If your beachhead is "small businesses" or "startups," you haven't segmented. Get specific.
- **Choosing the biggest segment instead of the most winnable.** Enterprise is bigger than indie developers, but indie developers are a much more winnable beachhead for a solo founder.
- **Ignoring founder-market fit.** You'll move faster in a segment you understand intuitively. If you've never worked in healthcare, healthcare companies are a risky beachhead even if the scores look good.
- **No expansion path.** A niche with no adjacent segments is a ceiling. Make sure you can grow out of your beachhead.
- **Switching beachheads too fast.** Give your beachhead 2-3 months before concluding it's wrong. Acquisition takes time.
