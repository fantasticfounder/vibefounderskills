# Build Your Objection Playbook

Prepare for the objections you'll face when selling your product - and learn to handle them without feeling like a used car salesman. This skill builds a ready-to-use playbook for the most common pushback founders hear, using response frameworks rooted in empathy, not manipulation. If you hate selling, this skill is specifically for you.

## Context

You are helping a founder build an objection handling playbook for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides pricing strategy, offer creation, positioning work, sales page copy, or any prior context from this session, read those first and use them. Objections are often about gaps in the offer or positioning - prior work reveals what those gaps might be.

## The Frameworks

This skill draws on two established methodologies:

- **Tactical empathy** - Chris Voss, "Never Split the Difference" (2016). Voss, a former FBI hostage negotiator, teaches that the most effective way to handle resistance is to acknowledge and label the other person's emotions before addressing the substance. "It sounds like you're worried about..." disarms defensiveness in a way that arguing never does. The core techniques: labeling (naming emotions), mirroring (repeating key words), and calibrated questions ("How would you like me to...").

- **Consultative selling** - an approach where the salesperson acts as an advisor, not a pitcher. The goal is to understand the buyer's problem deeply enough to recommend the right solution - which might not be your product. Attributed broadly to the solution selling movement (Mack Hanan, "Consultative Selling," 1970) and widely practiced in modern B2B SaaS sales.

**Why these frameworks for vibecoders:** Most founders who hate sales hate it because they picture aggressive pitching. Tactical empathy and consultative selling are the opposite - they're about listening, understanding, and genuinely helping. If you can do customer support, you can do sales with these frameworks.

### Step 1: Identify Product-Specific Objections

Every product has its own objection profile. Start by identifying the most likely objections by category:

**Price objections:**
- "It's too expensive"
- "I can't afford it right now"
- "There's a free alternative"
- "[Competitor] is cheaper"
- "I can build this myself"

**Trust objections:**
- "I've never heard of you"
- "How do I know it works?"
- "It's too new / not mature enough"
- "What if you shut down?"
- "Where's the social proof?"

**Need objections:**
- "I don't need this right now"
- "I'm not sure it'll work for my use case"
- "I can do this manually"
- "I need to talk to my [partner/co-founder/team]"

**Timing objections:**
- "I'll think about it"
- "Not right now, maybe later"
- "After my next funding round / after launch / after [milestone]"
- "I'm too busy to set this up right now"

Based on the specific product, prioritise the 5-8 objections most likely to come up. Consider:
- If it's a new category, trust objections dominate
- If there are established competitors, price and differentiation objections dominate
- If the product is a "nice to have" rather than a "must have," need and timing objections dominate

If you have pricing, offer, or positioning work from this session, use it to predict which objections are most likely. A product with no free tier will get "There's a free alternative" more often. A new unknown product will get "I've never heard of you."

**Research real objections (Web Search):**

Search for what actual users and prospects say about products in this space:

- `"[product type]" reviews site:g2.com OR site:capterra.com` - find real user complaints and hesitations
- `reddit "[product type]" "too expensive" OR "not worth" OR "I can build"` - find real objections in discussions
- `"[competitor]" complaints OR "switched from"` - see why people leave competitor products
- `"[product type]" "I'll just use" OR "free alternative"` - find the specific alternatives people compare to

Real objections from real users are more accurate than predicted objections. Use what you find to prioritise the objection list above.

### Step 2: Build Response Frameworks

For each of the top 5-8 objections, provide:

**The objection:** [exact words the customer says]

**The real objection:** What they actually mean (the objection behind the objection). "It's too expensive" usually means "I don't see enough value to justify the cost" - not that they literally can't afford it.

**Response framework: Acknowledge - Explore - Reframe - Bridge**

1. **Acknowledge**: Validate their concern without agreeing or disagreeing. Use tactical empathy (Voss): label the emotion. "It sounds like you're not sure the investment would pay off for you." This is NOT "I understand" (dismissive) or "But..." (argumentative).

2. **Explore**: Ask a calibrated question (Voss) to understand the real concern. "What would it need to do for you to feel confident it was worth the price?" or "Help me understand - what are you comparing it to?" These questions start with "what" or "how" - never "why" (which feels accusatory).

3. **Reframe**: Shift the frame from cost to value, from risk to opportunity, or from "now vs later" to "cost of waiting." "If this saves you 5 hours a week and your time is worth $75/hour, the tool pays for itself in 3 days."

4. **Bridge**: Move to a next step that reduces risk. Not "So do you want to buy it?" - that creates pressure. Instead: "Would it help to try it for 14 days and see if the time savings are real for your workflow?" Always bridge to the lowest-commitment next action.

**Example response:** [Write a complete, natural-sounding response in conversational tone. Not corporate. Not salesy. Like a founder talking to another founder.]

### Step 3: Teach the Selling Mindset

This section is about the mental model, not tactics. Include these principles:

**Principle 1: You're not convincing - you're helping someone decide.**
Your job is not to persuade someone to buy something they don't need. Your job is to help them figure out if this solves their problem. If it does, you help them take the next step. If it doesn't, the right answer is "This probably isn't for you - here's what might work better." Turning away a bad-fit customer is the most trust-building thing you can do.

**Principle 2: If they object, they're interested.**
People who aren't interested don't object - they leave. An objection means they're considering it but something is in the way. Your job is to understand what's in the way and address it honestly.

**Principle 3: Ask questions, don't make pitches.**
The best salespeople talk less than the buyer. Ask: "What's the biggest pain point for you right now?" and "What have you tried before?" and "What would make this a clear yes for you?" These questions do more selling than any pitch.

**Principle 4: Tactical empathy works because it's real.**
Labeling someone's concern ("It sounds like you're worried about committing to another tool that might not stick") is not a trick. It's demonstrating that you listened and you care. People buy from people who understand their problem.

**Principle 5: The follow-up is where deals happen.**
80% of sales happen after the 5th follow-up. Most founders give up after 1. A simple "Hey, just checking in - did you get a chance to try it?" is not pushy. It's attentive. Set reminders. Follow up 3-5 times over 2-4 weeks. Then stop.

### Step 4: Channel-Specific Responses

Provide response templates for different channels:

**Email responses:**
For when someone replies to a cold outreach or marketing email with an objection. Keep it short (3-5 sentences). Format: acknowledge, one reframe, bridge to next step. No walls of text.

**DM responses (Twitter/X, Reddit, Discord):**
Even shorter (2-3 sentences). Match the platform's tone. Casual. No formal language. End with a question, not a pitch.

**Call/meeting scripts:**
More conversational. Include the pauses. "That's a really fair point. [Pause.] Can I ask what you're comparing it to?" Live conversations allow you to use Voss's mirroring technique - repeat the last 2-3 words they said as a question: "Build it yourself?"

**Landing page FAQ format:**
Turn each objection into a question and answer it in 2-3 sentences. Direct, confident, specific.

## Common Mistakes

- **Arguing with objections.** "No, it's actually not expensive because..." is arguing. "It sounds like the price feels high relative to what you've seen. What are you comparing it to?" is empathy. The first creates resistance. The second creates a conversation.
- **Treating all objections the same.** "I'll think about it" from someone who spent 30 minutes on a demo is different from "I'll think about it" from someone who glanced at your landing page for 10 seconds. Context matters.
- **Over-handling.** Not every objection needs a 5-minute response. Sometimes "Totally fair - here's a free trial link so you can see for yourself" is the best answer.
- **Ignoring the real objection.** When someone says "I can't afford it," they might mean "I don't see the value," "I need approval from someone else," or "I genuinely don't have the budget." Only exploring reveals which one it is.
- **Never following up.** One message is not selling. It's hoping. A respectful 3-5 touchpoint follow-up sequence is expected in business. You're not being pushy - you're being professional.
- **Selling to the wrong person.** If someone genuinely doesn't need your product, the best thing you can do is tell them. "Honestly, this probably isn't the right fit for what you described. You might want to look at [alternative]." This builds more trust than any sale.
