# Structure Your Sales Page

Build a landing page that converts visitors into users or customers. This skill gives you the section-by-section structure with actual copy guidance - not just "write a compelling headline" but what that headline should say and why. Built on proven copywriting frameworks adapted for technical founders who can build anything but have never written a word of sales copy.

## Context

You are helping a founder structure a sales page for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides positioning work, value proposition, pricing strategy, offer creation, or any prior context from this session, read those first and use them. The sales page should directly reflect prior positioning and offer decisions.

## The Frameworks

This skill draws on three established copywriting and messaging frameworks:

- **AIDA** (Attention - Interest - Desire - Action) - the foundational marketing funnel model. Attributed to Elias St. Elmo Lewis (1898). Still the backbone of every sales page: grab attention, build interest, create desire, prompt action.
- **PAS** (Problem - Agitate - Solution) - a copywriting formula for the problem section. Widely used in direct response copywriting. Describe the problem, make it feel urgent, then present the solution.
- **StoryBrand** - Donald Miller, "Building a StoryBrand" (2017). Positions the CUSTOMER as the hero and your PRODUCT as the guide. The customer has a problem, meets a guide (you), gets a plan, takes action, achieves success, and avoids failure. Every section of your sales page maps to this story arc.

**Why StoryBrand for technical founders:** Most developer landing pages read like feature specs. StoryBrand forces you to write from the customer's perspective. Your visitor doesn't care about your tech stack. They care about their problem and whether you can solve it.

### Step 1: Gather Inputs

Before writing anything, you need these inputs. Pull from prior session context if available:

1. **Product name**: What is it called?
2. **Target audience**: Who specifically is this page for? (from positioning or value-proposition work)
3. **Value proposition**: Why should they care? (from value-proposition skill if available)
4. **Key differentiator**: What makes you different from alternatives? (from positioning work)
5. **Pricing**: What does it cost? (from pricing-strategy if available)
6. **Social proof available**: Testimonials? User count? Logos? Beta feedback? Even "3 beta users love it" counts.
7. **Primary CTA**: What do you want visitors to do? (Sign up, start free trial, buy now, join waitlist)

If any of these are missing, ask the user. If pricing and offer work hasn't been done, note the gaps and provide placeholder sections.

**Research competitor landing pages (Web Search):**

Before writing copy, search for how competitors present their products:

- `"[competitor] landing page"` or visit competitor websites directly - study their hero copy, structure, and CTAs
- `"[product type] landing page examples"` - find curated examples in the space
- `"best SaaS landing pages [product type]"` - see what's considered best practice

Note: what competitors do well (steal the structure, not the words) and what they do badly (opportunities to differentiate).

### Step 2: Build the Page Structure

Write actual copy guidance for each section. Not "write a headline" - write what the headline should communicate and provide 2-3 options.

**Section 1: Hero**

The first thing visitors see. You have 3-5 seconds to communicate value.

- **Headline**: Benefit-driven, under 10 words. State the outcome, not the product. "Send invoices in 30 seconds" not "AI-Powered Invoice Generation Platform."
  - Formula 1: [Verb] + [desired outcome] + [without common pain] - "Build landing pages without writing code"
  - Formula 2: [Tool type] + [that/for] + [specific outcome] - "Analytics that tell you what to fix, not just what happened"
  - Formula 3: [Outcome] + [for who] - "Meeting notes on autopilot for busy founders"
  - Provide 2-3 headline options for the founder to test.

- **Subheadline**: 1-2 sentences. Who it's for + what it does in plain language. This is where you get specific: "A meeting notes tool that records, transcribes, and summarises your calls so you can focus on the conversation instead of scribbling notes."

- **Primary CTA**: One button. Action-oriented language. "Start free trial" not "Submit." "Try it free" not "Learn more." Reduce friction: if free trial, say "No credit card required."

- **Social proof snippet**: One line of proof. "Trusted by 500+ freelancers" or "4.8/5 on Product Hunt" or a one-line testimonial from a real person.

- **Hero image/visual**: Recommend what to show: product screenshot, short demo GIF, or illustration of the outcome. NOT a stock photo. NOT a generic graphic.

**Section 2: Problem (PAS)**

Make the visitor feel understood. If they don't see their problem reflected, they'll leave.

- **Problem**: Describe the problem in THEIR language. Not your language. If your users say "I waste hours on invoices" - use those words. Don't say "invoice workflow inefficiencies."
- **Agitate**: Make the problem feel real and urgent. What happens if they don't solve it? What's the cost of the status quo? "Every hour you spend on invoices is an hour you're not building. At 5 hours a week, that's 260 hours a year - over 32 full working days spent on admin instead of your actual work."
- **Transition**: Bridge to your solution: "There's a better way." or "What if you could..." Keep it one sentence.

**Section 3: Solution**

What your product does about the problem. Focus on outcomes, not features.

- Lead with the transformation: "With [product], you [outcome]."
- Keep it to 2-3 sentences max. The detail comes later.
- If you have a value proposition statement from prior work, this is where it lives.

**Section 4: How It Works**

3 steps. Maximum. If your product can't be explained in 3 steps, simplify the explanation (not the product).

- Step 1: [Simple action] - "Connect your calendar"
- Step 2: [Product does the work] - "We record and transcribe your meetings automatically"
- Step 3: [Customer gets the outcome] - "Get a summary and action items in your inbox"

Each step should be one sentence. Include an icon or screenshot per step if the founder can provide them.

**Section 5: Features and Benefits**

3-5 key features. Each one tied to a specific benefit and outcome. Use this format:

- **Feature name** - [What it does] so you can [benefit/outcome].
  - Example: "Auto-transcription - Records and transcribes every meeting so you never miss a detail, even when you're focused on the conversation."
  - Example: "Smart summaries - Turns hour-long meetings into 2-minute action items so your team knows exactly what to do next."

Do NOT list every feature. Pick the 3-5 that matter most to your primary audience. Save the full feature list for a separate features page or FAQ.

**Section 6: Social Proof**

Use what you have. Something is always better than nothing:

- **Best**: Named testimonials with photo, name, role, and specific result. "We saved 12 hours a week - Sarah K., Marketing Lead at Acme"
- **Good**: User counts, ratings, logos. "Used by 500+ teams" or "4.8/5 average rating"
- **Acceptable**: Beta feedback, early user quotes (with permission), Product Hunt results, Twitter praise
- **Minimum viable proof**: "Currently in beta with 20 teams" - even acknowledging early stage builds trust over claiming nothing

Format testimonials as quotes with attribution. 2-3 is enough. Quality over quantity.

**Section 7: Pricing**

Show your pricing clearly. Don't hide it or force people to "contact sales" unless you're selling enterprise.

- Show tier cards side by side
- Highlight the recommended tier (visual distinction + "Most Popular" badge)
- List what's included in each tier (brief, not exhaustive)
- Include the primary CTA on each tier card
- If annual billing is available, show the savings
- If you have a free tier or trial, make it prominent: "Start free - no credit card required"

Pull from pricing-strategy output if available. If pricing hasn't been decided, provide placeholder structure and note it needs to be filled in.

**Section 8: FAQ**

Address the top 5 objections as questions. These are not real questions - they're objections disguised as questions.

Common patterns:
- "How is this different from [alternative]?" - differentiation objection
- "What if I don't like it?" - risk objection (answer with guarantee)
- "Do I need [technical skill] to use it?" - complexity objection
- "Can I cancel anytime?" - commitment objection
- "Is my data safe?" - trust objection

If objection-handler work has been done in this session, pull the top 5 objections from there and format them as FAQ questions.

**Section 9: Final CTA**

Repeat the main call to action. The visitor has scrolled this far - they're interested.

- Restate the core value proposition in one sentence
- Include the CTA button (same as hero)
- Add urgency if real: "Launch pricing: $X/mo (goes up to $Y after [date])"
- Add guarantee if available: "30-day money-back guarantee. No questions asked."
- Do NOT use fake urgency (countdown timers with no real deadline, "only 3 spots left" when there's no limit)

### Step 3: Write Copy Recommendations

For each section, provide:
- The actual recommended copy (not just descriptions of what to write)
- 2-3 headline/CTA options where relevant
- A note on tone: conversational, clear, no jargon. Write like you're explaining to a smart friend, not pitching to a boardroom.

### Step 4: Identify Common Mistakes

Flag any issues specific to this product:
- **Too many CTAs**: One primary CTA per page. "Sign up," "Start trial," "Book a demo," and "Download the guide" on the same page dilutes everything.
- **Feature-heavy, benefit-light**: Technical founders list specs. Customers want outcomes.
- **No social proof**: Even early-stage proof (beta users, Product Hunt comments) is better than nothing.
- **Burying the price**: If visitors have to click through to find pricing, most won't. Show it on the page.
- **Jargon**: "Leveraging synergistic AI capabilities" means nothing. "Summarises your meetings in 30 seconds" means everything.
- **Wall of text**: Use short paragraphs. White space. Scannable headers. Most visitors skim before they read.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing for yourself instead of your customer.** Your landing page is not your portfolio. The visitor doesn't care what you built - they care what it does for them.
- **Starting with "We" instead of "You."** The hero section should be about the visitor's problem and outcome. "You" should appear before "We" on the page.
- **No clear visual hierarchy.** If everything is bold, nothing is bold. One headline per section. One CTA per section. Plenty of white space.
- **Multiple competing CTAs.** "Sign up," "Watch demo," "Read blog," "Download guide" all on the same page. Pick one primary action. Everything else is secondary.
- **Forgetting mobile.** Most visitors are on mobile. Long headlines break. Tier comparison tables don't fit. Design for mobile first.
