# Create Your Offer

Turn your product and price into an offer that makes people say "that's a no-brainer." An offer is not just a price - it's the total package of what you're selling, how you're framing it, and why the buyer feels like they'd be stupid to say no. This skill uses the value equation from Alex Hormozi's "$100M Offers" (2021), adapted for indie founders and vibecoders selling software, not gym memberships.

## Context

If the user provides pricing strategy, monetization model, positioning work, or any prior context from this session, read those first and use them. The offer should wrap around the pricing already decided.

## The Framework

The core framework comes from Alex Hormozi, "$100M Offers" (2021). Hormozi's Value Equation:

**Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)**

To increase value without changing the price:
- **Increase the Dream Outcome**: Make the end result bigger and more desirable
- **Increase Perceived Likelihood**: Make people believe they'll actually get the result
- **Decrease Time Delay**: Make them get the result faster
- **Decrease Effort & Sacrifice**: Make it easier for them to get the result

This is how a $49/month tool can feel more valuable than a $499/month tool - if the offer is structured right.

Risk reversal principles are drawn from direct response marketing tradition, formalised by Jay Abraham and widely applied in SaaS (free trials, money-back guarantees, satisfaction guarantees).

### Step 1: Identify the Dream Outcome

The dream outcome is not your product. It's not even what your product does. It's what the customer ultimately wants their life or business to look like AFTER using your product.

Ask or determine:
- What does the customer want to achieve? (Not "use your tool" - the real outcome)
- What would their life/work look like if this problem were perfectly solved?
- What would they brag about to a friend?

Examples:
- Meeting notes tool: The dream outcome is NOT "transcribed meetings." It's "never miss an action item, always look prepared, save 5 hours a week to focus on real work."
- Invoicing tool: The dream outcome is NOT "send invoices faster." It's "get paid on time, every time, without chasing clients."
- Analytics dashboard: The dream outcome is NOT "see your metrics." It's "know exactly what to fix next to grow your business."

If you have value-proposition work from this session, pull the "What After" section - that's your dream outcome.

**Research competitor offers (Web Search):**

Before building the value stack, search for what competitors include in their offers:

- `"[competitor] pricing"` or visit competitor pricing pages - see what's included at each tier
- `"[product type] bonus"` or `"[product type] onboarding"` - find common bonuses and add-ons in the space
- `"[product type] guarantee"` or `"[competitor] refund policy"` - see what risk reversals competitors use
- App store or G2/Capterra reviews: `"[competitor]" "wish it included"` - find gaps in competitor offers you can fill

Use this to inform the value stack design (Step 3) and identify bonuses that competitors don't offer.

### Step 2: Apply the Value Equation

Map each element of Hormozi's equation to your specific product and offer:

**Dream Outcome** (maximise this):
- What is the specific, tangible outcome?
- Can you quantify it? (hours saved, revenue gained, problems eliminated)
- How can you make this outcome feel bigger in the offer framing?

**Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** (maximise this):
- What proof do you have that this works? (testimonials, case studies, your own results)
- What can you add to the offer that increases confidence? (templates, guides, community, onboarding)
- What reduces the perceived risk of failure? (guarantee, support, "done-for-you" elements)

**Time Delay** (minimise this):
- How quickly does the customer get the first result?
- What can you add to the offer that speeds things up? (quick-start template, setup wizard, migration tool)
- Can you promise a specific time to first value? ("Set up in 5 minutes" or "See your first report in 24 hours")

**Effort & Sacrifice** (minimise this):
- How much work does the customer have to do?
- What can you add to reduce their effort? (auto-import, integrations, concierge onboarding)
- What do they have to give up? (time to learn, switching costs, data migration pain)

### Step 3: Build the Value Stack

The value stack is everything included in the offer. The core product is just one component. The bonuses are what make the offer feel like a steal.

**Core product:** [what they're buying]

**Bonus 1 - Reduces time delay:**
Something that gets them to the result faster. Examples: quick-start template, setup guide, pre-built configurations, migration tool. Put a dollar value on this. What would it cost them to create this themselves or hire someone to do it?

**Bonus 2 - Reduces effort:**
Something that makes it easier. Examples: video walkthrough, cheat sheet, done-for-you setup, integration with their existing tools. Value this too.

**Bonus 3 - Increases likelihood:**
Something that increases their confidence they'll succeed. Examples: community access (others who've done it), 1:1 onboarding call (first 50 customers), case study library, best practices guide. Value this.

**Bonus 4 (optional) - Increases dream outcome:**
Something that makes the outcome even bigger. Examples: advanced training, premium templates, strategy call. Value this.

Rules for bonuses:
- Every bonus must genuinely help the customer succeed. No fluff.
- Each bonus should be something you can actually deliver (don't promise a community of 10,000 when you have 10 users)
- Value each bonus individually. Be honest but generous - what would someone pay for this standalone?
- Digital products (templates, guides, videos) are ideal bonuses because they cost you nothing per customer once created

### Step 4: Set the Price Anchor

Now show the total value before revealing the price:

"Here's everything you get:
- [Core product] - ($X value)
- [Bonus 1] - ($X value)
- [Bonus 2] - ($X value)
- [Bonus 3] - ($X value)
- Total value: $[sum]
- Your price: $[price]"

The gap between total value and price is what makes the offer feel like a no-brainer. A good ratio: total value should be 5-10x the price.

Pull the actual price from pricing-strategy work if available. If not, recommend a price based on the value stack.

### Step 5: Add Risk Reversal

Risk reversal removes the fear of making a wrong decision. The stronger your guarantee, the more confident buyers feel.

**Types of risk reversal (from weakest to strongest):**

1. **Free trial** (weakest standalone): "Try it free for 14 days." Good for SaaS. Make sure the trial is long enough to experience the core value.
2. **Money-back guarantee**: "If you're not happy within 30 days, get a full refund. No questions asked." This is the standard for digital products. Refund rates for good products are typically 2-5% - you lose almost nothing.
3. **Conditional guarantee**: "If you [use the product as intended for X days] and don't [get the promised result], we'll refund you AND [additional compensation]." Stronger because it's specific.
4. **Better-than-money-back guarantee** (strongest): "If this doesn't [deliver specific result] in [timeframe], I'll refund you AND give you [something extra]." Example: "If you don't save at least 5 hours in your first month, I'll refund you and give you a free hour of consulting."

Pick the guarantee that matches your confidence in the product. The stronger the guarantee, the more it sells. Most founders underestimate how few people actually request refunds.

### Step 6: Create Urgency (Real, Not Fake)

Real urgency drives action. Fake urgency destroys trust.

**Real urgency mechanisms:**
- **Launch pricing**: "Founding member price: $29/mo. Goes up to $49/mo on [date]." This is real if you actually raise the price.
- **Early adopter benefits**: "First 100 customers get lifetime access to [bonus]." Real scarcity if you actually cap it.
- **Limited spots for high-touch elements**: "Includes a 1:1 onboarding call - only available for the first 50 customers." Real if you only have capacity for 50 calls.
- **Seasonal relevance**: "Launch before Q2" or "Set up before tax season." Real if timing matters.

**Fake urgency to avoid:**
- Countdown timers that reset when the page reloads
- "Only 3 spots left" when there's no actual limit
- "This offer expires today" every day
- Fake scarcity on a digital product with unlimited inventory

Customers are not stupid. Fake urgency gets clicks but kills trust and increases refunds.

### Step 7: Write the Offer Summary

Condense everything into one paragraph that makes someone say "that's a no-brainer." This paragraph should work as:
- The offer section of your sales page
- The pitch in a sales conversation
- The summary in an email

Structure: "[Product name] gives you [dream outcome] by [how]. You get [core product] plus [key bonuses] - a total value of [$X] - for just [$price]. And if [guarantee condition], [risk reversal]. [Urgency if applicable]."

## Common Mistakes

- **No bonuses.** Just listing a product and a price is not an offer. It's a price tag. Bonuses that reduce time, effort, and risk are what make the price feel small.
- **Fake bonuses.** "Bonus: Access to our dashboard!" That's... the product. Bonuses should be additional things that help the customer succeed, not features you were going to include anyway.
- **Overvaluing bonuses.** Don't claim a PDF template is worth $997. Customers can smell inflated values. Be generous but honest. A template you spent 10 hours building? $200-500 is fair. Not $2,000.
- **No risk reversal.** If you believe in your product, put a guarantee on it. "But what if everyone asks for a refund?" They won't. Refund rates on quality products are 2-5%. The increase in conversions from a guarantee far outweighs the refund cost.
- **Fake urgency.** The fastest way to destroy trust. If you say "offer ends Friday," it better actually end Friday.
- **Copying high-ticket offer structures for low-ticket products.** A $49/month SaaS doesn't need a 47-minute VSL with 12 bonuses and a "fast-action bonus." Keep it proportional to the price.
