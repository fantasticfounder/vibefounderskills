# Craft Your Elevator Pitch

Build a clear pitch for your product that works in real conversations - from a one-sentence intro to a 60-second explanation. Nobody remembers a feature list. People remember a story.

## Context

You are helping a founder craft their pitch for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides positioning work, value propositions, or product details, read those first. The pitch should be consistent with existing positioning.

## Why Most Pitches Fail

The typical founder pitch: "It's an AI-powered platform that leverages machine learning to optimise workflows and deliver actionable insights through an intuitive dashboard."

What the listener hears: nothing. They've already stopped listening.

**What works instead:**
- Lead with the problem, not the product
- Use concrete language, not abstractions
- Name the audience specifically
- Describe the outcome, not the technology
- Make it conversational, not performative

## Instructions

### Step 1: Gather the Ingredients

You need four things to build a good pitch:

1. **The audience** (from positioning work): Who specifically is this for?
2. **The problem** (from value proposition): What frustrates them?
3. **The solution** (your product): What does it do about it?
4. **The outcome** (the transformation): What changes for them?

If the user hasn't done positioning or value proposition work, help them answer these questions before writing the pitch.

### Step 2: Build Three Pitch Lengths

Write the pitch in three formats, each building on the previous:

**Format 1: The One-Liner (5-10 seconds)**

Structure: "[Product name] helps [specific audience] [achieve specific outcome]."

Rules:
- Under 15 words
- No jargon, no buzzwords
- A stranger should understand it immediately
- It should make someone say "oh, tell me more" or "I know someone who needs that"

Example: "Clarity helps freelancers send professional invoices in under 60 seconds."

NOT: "Clarity is an AI-powered invoicing platform that streamlines financial workflows."

**Format 2: The Problem-Solution (15-30 seconds)**

Structure:
1. [One sentence: the problem your audience faces]
2. [One sentence: what your product does about it]
3. [One sentence: the key outcome or differentiator]

Rules:
- Lead with the problem - it creates empathy and recognition
- Name the audience in the problem statement
- The solution should feel like an obvious response to the problem
- End with what makes you different, not just what you do

Example:
"Freelance designers spend hours every week creating and chasing invoices instead of doing client work. Clarity lets you create and send a professional invoice in under 60 seconds - it auto-generates from your project tracker. Unlike FreshBooks, there's zero setup and no learning curve. Want to try it free?"

**Format 3: The Story Pitch (45-60 seconds)**

Structure:
1. [Context: a relatable situation] (5 seconds)
2. [Problem: what's broken] (10 seconds)
3. [Turning point: your insight or discovery] (10 seconds)
4. [Solution: what you built] (10 seconds)
5. [Outcome: what's different now] (10 seconds)
6. [Proof: one data point or testimonial] (5 seconds)
7. [Ask: what you want from the listener] (5 seconds)

Rules:
- Tell a story, don't present a product
- Make it personal ("I was freelancing when I noticed..." or "A friend of mine kept complaining about...")
- Include ONE specific number or proof point
- End with a clear next step (try it, check it out, give me feedback)

If the founder hasn't shared their personal connection to the problem, ask: "What's your personal experience with this problem?" If no personal story exists, use a representative user story: "A [audience member] we spoke to was spending [X hours/dollars] on [problem]..."

Example:
"When I was freelancing, I noticed I was spending 3 hours every Friday doing invoices instead of client work. I tried FreshBooks, Wave, even Excel - they all required too much setup for someone with 5 clients. So I built Clarity - it connects to your project tracker and auto-generates invoices. One click, 60 seconds, done. My beta users are saving an average of 2.5 hours per week. Want to try it?"

### Step 3: Context-Specific Variations

Adapt the pitch for different situations:

| Context | Length | Emphasis | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Casual conversation** ("What do you do?") | One-liner | Simple, relatable | Conversational |
| **Networking event** | Problem-solution | Problem resonance | Engaging |
| **Reddit / community post** | Story pitch | Personal story, value | Authentic, not salesy |
| **Twitter / social bio** | One-liner (modified) | Hook + clarity | Punchy |
| **Landing page headline** | One-liner to problem-solution | Outcome-focused | Direct |
| **Cold outreach** | Problem-solution | Recipient's problem | Personalised |
| **Investor / accelerator** | Story pitch (modified) | Market + traction | Data-backed |

### Step 4: Test and Refine

Three tests for your pitch:

**The "So What?" Test** (AI-evaluatable): After each sentence, chain "so what?" If you can't connect it to why the listener should care, cut or rewrite it. Run this test now and include results in the output.

**The Repetition Test** (human-testable): Tell 3 people your one-liner. Can they repeat it back accurately? If not, it's too complex. Mark as "Founder to test - results TBD" in the output.

**The "Who's It For?" Test** (human-testable): After hearing your pitch, can someone name exactly who would use it? If they say "anyone" or "lots of people," the audience isn't specific enough. Mark as "Founder to test - results TBD" in the output.

## Common Mistakes

- **Starting with your product name.** Nobody cares about your product name until they care about their problem. Lead with the problem.
- **Using jargon.** "AI-powered," "leverages machine learning," "streamlines workflows" - these mean nothing to most people. Use words a 12-year-old would understand.
- **No specificity.** "Saves time" is generic. "Saves 3 hours per week on invoicing" is specific and memorable.
- **Pitching features instead of outcomes.** "We have automated templates, recurring billing, and PDF export" vs "You create and send an invoice in 60 seconds." Same product, different pitch. The second one works.
- **No ask at the end.** Every pitch should end with a next step. "Want to try it?" "Can I show you?" "Here's the link."

## Notes

- Your pitch should evolve as your product and understanding of your audience evolves
- Practice saying it out loud. A pitch that reads well but sounds awkward when spoken needs rewriting.
- The best pitches feel like conversations, not presentations
- This skill works best after positioning-canvas and value-proposition have established the strategic foundation
