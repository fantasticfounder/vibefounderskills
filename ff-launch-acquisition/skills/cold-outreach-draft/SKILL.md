# Draft Cold Outreach Messages

Write outreach messages that get responses instead of getting ignored. This skill covers the psychology of cold outreach, message structure, personalisation, and follow-up sequences.

## Context

You are helping a founder write cold outreach for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides details about their product, target audience, or prior outreach attempts, read those first.

## Why Cold Outreach Works (When Done Right)

Cold outreach has the highest conversion rate of any acquisition channel for B2B and high-value products. But most founders do it wrong: generic messages, no personalisation, unclear ask, too salesy.

The principles that separate good outreach from spam:

**1. Relevance > Volume.** 10 personalised messages outperform 200 templates. The recipient should feel you wrote this for THEM, not for a list.

**2. Value before ask.** Lead with something useful - an observation, a compliment, a resource. The ask comes second.

**3. One ask per message.** "Would you be open to trying this?" is one ask. "Check out our app, share with your team, and schedule a demo" is three asks disguised as one.

**4. Reciprocity principle.** People respond to people who give first. If you can offer genuine value before asking for anything, response rates jump.

**5. Social proof reduces friction.** "I built this to solve a problem I noticed in [their industry]" is more credible than "I built an amazing tool."

### Step 1: Define Outreach Parameters

Ask the user (if not already clear):
- Who exactly are you reaching out to? (job title, industry, company size)
- What channel? (email, LinkedIn DM, Twitter DM, community message, Reddit DM)
- What's the ask? (try the product, give feedback, have a short call, something else)
- What's the product's value prop for THIS specific recipient?
- Do you have any existing relationship or common ground with recipients?

### Step 2: Research the Recipient Pattern

Before writing, identify what you can personalise:
- **Company-level**: What does their company do? Recent news, funding, product launches?
- **Role-level**: What problems does someone in this role face daily?
- **Individual-level**: Recent posts, articles, tweets, open source contributions?
- **Trigger events**: Did they just hire for a related role? Launch a new feature? Post about a problem your product solves?

The more specific your personalisation, the higher the response rate. "I saw your tweet about X" beats "I noticed you work in Y industry."

### Step 3: Choose a Message Framework

**Framework 1: Problem-Solution (best for products that solve an obvious pain)**

```
[Personalised observation about their specific situation]

I noticed [specific problem signal]. I built [product] that [specific solution to that problem].

[One sentence of social proof or result if you have it]

Would you be open to trying it? Happy to give you [free access / extended trial / whatever].
```

**Framework 2: Mutual Interest (best when you have common ground)**

```
[Reference to shared context - community, mutual connection, similar work]

I've been working on [product] that [what it does]. Given your work on [their thing], I thought you might find it useful.

[Specific reason this is relevant to them]

Would love your take on it - even 5 minutes of feedback would be valuable.
```

**Framework 3: Give-First (best for building long-term relationships)**

```
[Personalised observation]

I came across [their work/company/post] and thought you might find this useful: [free resource, insight, or tool that's genuinely valuable - NOT your product pitch].

[Brief mention of what you do, naturally connected to the value you just gave]

No ask - just thought it'd be helpful. If you ever want to chat about [topic], I'm around.
```

**Framework 4: Feedback Request (best for early-stage products)**

```
[Brief context about you and what you're building]

I'm building [product] for [audience]. I've seen your [work/posts/company] and you seem like someone who'd have a sharp perspective on this.

Would you be open to a 10-minute call or async feedback? I'd genuinely value your input - not pitching, just learning.
```

### Step 4: Write Channel-Specific Versions

Adapt the message for the channel:

| Channel | Length | Tone | Tips |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cold email** | 50-100 words | Professional but human | Clear subject line, one CTA, easy to reply to |
| **LinkedIn DM** | 30-60 words | Conversational | Reference their profile, don't pitch in the connection request |
| **Twitter/X DM** | 20-40 words | Casual | Very short, reference a specific tweet or post |
| **Community message** | 40-80 words | Community-native | Use the community's language, reference shared context |

### Step 5: Design a Follow-Up Sequence

Most responses come from follow-ups, not the first message. Design a 3-touch sequence:

**Touch 1 (Day 0)**: Initial outreach message
**Touch 2 (Day 3-4)**: Brief follow-up. Add new value or a different angle. Don't just say "bumping this up."
**Touch 3 (Day 7-10)**: Final follow-up. Be direct: "Totally understand if this isn't relevant. Just wanted to make sure it didn't get buried. Last ping from me!"

After 3 touches with no response, move on. Persistence is good. Being annoying is bad.

### Step 6: Build Your Recipient List

Before sending outreach, you need to find recipients. Where to look:

- **LinkedIn search**: Filter by title, industry, company size to find people matching your target profile
- **Apollo.io or Hunter.io**: Find verified email addresses for your target recipients
- **Twitter/X bio search**: Search for keywords matching your audience (e.g., "freelance designer" or "SaaS founder" in bios)
- **ProductHunt**: Find makers in your space by browsing recent launches in your product category
- **Relevant subreddit post authors**: People who post about the problem you solve are warm leads

Aim for a list of 30-50 qualified recipients before starting outreach. Quality of list matters more than quality of message.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing a novel.** Cold messages should be 50-100 words max. If it's longer, cut it in half.
- **Making it about you.** "I built this amazing thing" vs "I noticed you have this problem" - the second works.
- **No personalisation.** If you could send the exact same message to 100 people, it's spam. If you couldn't, it's outreach.
- **Unclear ask.** "Let me know what you think" is vague. "Would you be open to a 10-minute call this week?" is clear.
- **Giving up after one message.** 80% of responses come from follow-ups. Design the sequence, not just the first message.
- **Pitching in the first sentence.** Build context first, pitch second. Or don't pitch at all - just offer value.

## Notes

- Cold outreach works best for B2B products, high-value services, and niche tools where each user is worth the effort
- For low-price consumer products, community marketing and content are usually better ROI than outreach
- Quality of list > quality of message > volume of sends
- The ethical line: personalised outreach to relevant people = fine. Mass-blasting irrelevant people = spam.
