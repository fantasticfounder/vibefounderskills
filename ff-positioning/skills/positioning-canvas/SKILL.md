# Positioning Canvas

Define exactly how your product is different and why that matters - using April Dunford's positioning framework from "Obviously Awesome" (2019). Positioning is the context you set for your product so that customers immediately understand what it is, who it's for, and why it's better than alternatives.

## Context

You are helping a founder position their product: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides details about their product, competitors, customers, or market research, read those first.

## What Positioning Is (And Isn't)

**Positioning IS**: The deliberate choice of context that makes your product's strengths obvious. It answers: "What is this, who is it for, and why should they care?"

**Positioning is NOT**: A tagline, a slogan, or marketing copy. Those come AFTER positioning. Positioning is the strategic foundation that all messaging is built on.

Bad positioning example: "An AI-powered productivity platform for modern teams."
- What does it actually DO? Unclear.
- Who are "modern teams"? Everyone.
- Why is AI-powered relevant? Not explained.

Good positioning example: "A meeting summariser for freelance consultants who waste 3 hours/week writing meeting notes. Unlike Otter.ai, we generate client-ready summaries with action items - not raw transcripts."
- Clear what it does (meeting summariser)
- Clear who it's for (freelance consultants)
- Clear why it's better (client-ready summaries vs raw transcripts)
- Named the alternative (Otter.ai) - forces clarity

### Step 1: April Dunford's 5 Positioning Components

Work through these in order. Each component builds on the previous one.

**Component 1: Competitive Alternatives**

What would your customers use if your product didn't exist? NOT just direct competitors - include all alternatives:
- Other products in the same category
- Manual processes (spreadsheets, pen and paper, doing it by hand)
- Hiring someone (freelancer, agency, employee)
- Doing nothing (living with the problem)
- Workarounds (duct-tape solutions using other tools not designed for this)

List 3-5 realistic alternatives. Be honest about what customers actually consider.

If the founder is unsure of competitors, use web search. Search: "[product type] alternatives", "[product type] vs", "[audience] tools". Do not rely on memory alone for competitor research in fast-moving markets.

If you are pre-revenue, the alternatives you list are hypotheses. April Dunford emphasises that competitive alternatives should ideally be discovered from actual customers. Flag this: "These alternatives are our best hypothesis - validate with real customer conversations."

**Component 2: Unique Attributes**

What features or capabilities do you have that alternatives DON'T? These must be:
- **Real**: Actually exist in your product today, not on a roadmap
- **Unique**: Alternatives genuinely don't have this (or do it much worse)
- **Provable**: You can demonstrate this, not just claim it

List each unique attribute. For each, note which alternatives specifically lack it.

**Component 3: Value (So What?)**

For each unique attribute, translate it into customer value. Customers don't care about features - they care about outcomes.

| Unique Attribute | Value for Customer |
|---|---|
| [feature/capability] | [what the customer gains: time saved, money earned, risk reduced, problem eliminated] |

The "so what?" test: if a customer hears your feature and says "so what?", you haven't connected it to value.

**Component 4: Target Customer Characteristics**

Who are the customers that care MOST about the value you deliver? Not everyone - the specific people for whom your unique attributes matter the most.

Define them by:
- What situation are they in? (not demographics - situations)
- What have they tried before?
- What frustrated them about alternatives?
- What would make them switch?

**Component 5: Market Category**

What category does your product belong to? The category sets customer expectations for features, pricing, competitors, and use cases.

Three category strategies:
- **Existing category**: "We're a [category] that does [differentiator]." Works when the category is well-understood and you have a clear advantage.
- **Subcategory**: "We're the first [qualifier] + [category]." Works when you're creating a new segment within an existing category. Subcategory example: "meeting notes tool" is the existing category. "Async-first meeting tool for distributed teams" is a subcategory - it inherits the expectations of the parent category but carves out a specific position.
- **New category**: You're creating something that doesn't have an established name. Risky - you have to educate the market on what the category even IS.

For most solo founders, **existing category with a differentiator** is the safest choice. New categories require massive marketing budgets to establish.

### Step 2: Write the Positioning Statement

Combine the 5 components into a structured positioning statement:

**For** [target customer characteristics]
**who** [key problem or situation]
**[product name] is a** [market category]
**that** [key value / what it does for them].
**Unlike** [primary competitive alternative],
**we** [key unique attribute + value].

### Step 3: Test the Positioning

Stress-test the positioning with these checks:

| Test | Question | Pass/Fail |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Can someone who's never heard of your product understand what it does in 10 seconds? | |
| **Specificity** | Does the positioning name a specific audience and alternative? Or is it generic? | |
| **Truthfulness** | Can you deliver on every claim? Are unique attributes real and provable? | |
| **Differentiation** | If you replaced your product name with a competitor's, would the statement still be true? (It shouldn't be.) | |
| **Relevance** | Does the target customer actually care about the value you highlight? | |

If any test fails, revise the relevant positioning component and rewrite the statement. Do not output a failing positioning statement as the final deliverable. Iterate until all 5 tests pass, or flag which tests cannot be resolved without more customer data.

## Common Mistakes

- **Positioning against "everyone" instead of specific alternatives.** "We're better than existing solutions" is meaningless. Name the alternatives.
- **Listing features instead of value.** "We have AI" is a feature. "We save you 3 hours/week on meeting notes" is value.
- **Claiming uniqueness you don't have.** If a competitor has the same feature, it's not unique. Be brutally honest.
- **Choosing the wrong category.** If customers don't understand what category you're in, they don't know how to evaluate you, what to expect, or what to compare you to.
- **Positioning for who you WISH your customers were.** Position for your actual best customers - the ones who love your product and would be devastated if it disappeared.
