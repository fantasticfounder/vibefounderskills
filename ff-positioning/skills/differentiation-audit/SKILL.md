# Differentiation Audit

Find what makes your product different from alternatives - even when you think the answer is "nothing." Most products have differentiation they can't see because they're too close to it.

## Context

You are helping a founder find their differentiation for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides competitor research, customer feedback, product details, or positioning work, read those first.

## Why Founders Think They Have No Differentiation

The "everyone is building the same thing" problem is real in competitive spaces. But true undifferentiated products are rare. What's usually happening:

1. **You're comparing features, not experiences.** Two products can have the same features but wildly different experiences (speed, simplicity, design, onboarding).
2. **You're looking at the wrong competitive axis.** You might not be the fastest, but you might be the simplest. You might not be the cheapest, but you might be the best for a specific audience.
3. **You're ignoring non-product differentiation.** Your story, your audience focus, your support quality, your content, your community - these all differentiate.
4. **You're comparing yourself to the market leader.** Compare yourself to what YOUR specific audience uses today (which might be a spreadsheet, not Salesforce).

### Step 2: Analyse Differentiation Across 8 Dimensions

Don't just look at features. Examine differentiation across all dimensions:

**1. Product Differentiation**
- Features competitors don't have (or do poorly)
- Technical approach (architecture, speed, reliability)
- Design quality and user experience
- Integrations and ecosystem
- AI or automation capabilities

**2. Audience Differentiation**
- Are you built specifically for a niche that competitors ignore?
- Do you understand their language, workflow, and context better?
- Are you the ONLY product designed for this specific audience?

**3. Experience Differentiation**
- Is your product faster to learn, set up, or get value from?
- Is the onboarding dramatically simpler?
- Is the overall experience more enjoyable?

**4. Price Differentiation**
- Are you significantly cheaper? (Caution: don't race to zero)
- Is your pricing model better aligned with customer value? (e.g., per-use vs per-seat)
- Do you offer a generous free tier that competitors don't?

**5. Distribution Differentiation**
- Are you available on platforms competitors aren't?
- Do you have a unique distribution channel? (embedded in a tool, marketplace, API)
- Is your product easier to discover? (SEO, community presence)

**6. Content / Education Differentiation**
- Do you teach your customers something competitors don't?
- Is your content (blog, docs, guides) significantly better?
- Are you building a brand, not just a product?

**7. Support / Relationship Differentiation**
- Is your support faster, more personal, more helpful?
- As a solo founder, can you offer 1:1 attention that scaled competitors can't?
- Do you build relationships with users that create loyalty?

**8. Story / Mission Differentiation**
- Why does your product exist? What's the story?
- Are you solving a problem you personally experienced?
- Is there a mission or belief that resonates with your audience?

For each dimension, rate your differentiation: **Strong** (clear, provable advantage), **Moderate** (some advantage, not overwhelming), **None** (same as alternatives), or **Weakness** (competitors are better).

### Step 3: Blue Ocean Strategy Analysis

Apply Blue Ocean thinking to find differentiation opportunities:

**Four Actions Framework** (from "Blue Ocean Strategy" by W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne, 2004):

| Action | Question | Your Answer |
|---|---|---|
| **ELIMINATE** | What factors that the industry competes on can you eliminate entirely? | [what competitors offer that your audience doesn't actually need] |
| **REDUCE** | What factors can you reduce well below the industry standard? | [what you can offer less of without losing value for your specific audience] |
| **RAISE** | What factors should you raise well above the industry standard? | [what matters most to your audience that competitors under-deliver on] |
| **CREATE** | What factors can you create that the industry has never offered? | [what's completely new - a capability, experience, or approach nobody offers] |

The best differentiation often comes from **eliminating complexity** that competitors assume is necessary. Basecamp eliminated most project management features and won by being simpler. Notion eliminated the need for multiple tools by combining them.

### Step 5: Synthesise the Differentiation

Identify your **Decisive Differentiator** - the ONE thing that matters most:

Not all differentiation is equal. You need ONE primary differentiator that:
- Matters deeply to your target audience
- Is hard for competitors to replicate
- You can communicate in one sentence

When choosing between differentiators, prioritise the one with the highest barrier to replication (proprietary data > network effects > founder expertise > feature advantage). Feature advantages are easiest to copy.

## Common Mistakes

- **Only looking at features.** Features are the easiest dimension to copy. The hardest to copy: audience understanding, content depth, community trust, founder story.
- **Comparing yourself to the wrong competitor.** Compare against what YOUR customers actually use, not the market leader you aspire to beat.
- **Trying to differentiate on everything.** You need to be exceptional on 2-3 things, not marginally better on 10 things.
- **Ignoring the "doing nothing" competitor.** For many products, the biggest competitor isn't another app - it's the customer deciding the problem isn't worth solving.

## Notes

- This skill pairs with positioning-canvas (which turns differentiation into a positioning statement) and find-niche (which identifies WHERE to differentiate)
- Differentiation is not static. Competitors copy features. Continuously invest in the dimensions that are hardest to copy.
- Sometimes the audit reveals you genuinely don't have meaningful differentiation. That's valuable information - it means you need to either build it or find a different niche.
