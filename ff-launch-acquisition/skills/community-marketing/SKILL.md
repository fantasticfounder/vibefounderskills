# Community Marketing Strategy

Build a real presence in the communities where your target audience hangs out. This isn't about dropping links and running - it's about becoming a trusted member who happens to have a product they need.

If the user provides details about their product, audience, communities they've tried, or past results, read those first.

## Why Community Marketing Works

Communities are the highest-trust acquisition channel. When someone recommends a tool in a subreddit or Slack group, it carries more weight than any ad because:
- **Trust transfer**: Community members trust other members' recommendations
- **Problem-solution proximity**: People discuss problems openly in communities, creating natural entry points for solutions
- **Low cost, high intent**: Community members who convert are often the most engaged and loyal users
- **Compounding returns**: A reputation built over months continues paying dividends

The tradeoff: it's slow. Community marketing is a 3-6 month play, not a launch-day tactic. But the users it produces are higher quality than almost any other channel.

### Step 1: Map the Community Landscape

For each community type (Reddit, Discord, forums), use web search to find current active communities. Search: '[audience] community Reddit', '[audience] Discord server', '[audience] forum 2026'. Prioritise communities with 1K-50K members over mega-communities - niche is better for trust-building.

Identify ALL communities where your beachhead segment participates. Think broadly:

**Reddit**
- Niche subreddits specific to your audience's industry or interest
- Problem-focused subreddits where people discuss the pain your product solves
- Meta subreddits where your audience discovers tools (r/SideProject, r/SaaS, r/startups, etc.)

**Discord / Slack**
- Industry-specific servers (developer communities, designer communities, niche groups)
- Product ecosystem communities (e.g., Shopify Partners Discord, Webflow community)
- Paid communities (often higher quality, members are more invested)

**Forums / Niche Platforms**
- IndieHackers (for startup/SaaS builders)
- Hacker News (for technical products)
- Stack Overflow (for developer tools)
- Quora (for problem-specific questions)
- Industry-specific forums (e.g., Warrior Forum for marketers, BiggerPockets for real estate)

**Social Groups**
- Facebook Groups (strong for consumer, SMB, and niche audiences)
- LinkedIn Groups (strong for B2B)
- Twitter/X communities and lists

For each community, note:
- Size (members/subscribers)
- Activity level (posts per day/week)
- Rules about self-promotion
- Tone and culture (technical, casual, strict, welcoming)
- Whether your target audience is there (not just people interested in the topic)

### Step 2: Audit Community Rules and Culture

Before posting ANYTHING, study the community:

**Read the rules.** Many communities have explicit self-promotion rules. Violating them gets you banned and burns the channel permanently.

**Observe for 1-2 weeks.** What gets upvoted? What gets removed? What tone do successful posts use? Who are the respected members?

**Understand the power structure.** Who moderates? What's their stance on product mentions? Are there designated spaces for sharing what you've built?

**Categorise each community:**

| Type | Self-Promotion Policy | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Promotion-friendly** | Dedicated threads, "show off" days, share-your-project channels | Post about your product in designated spaces. Contribute elsewhere too. |
| **Value-first** | Self-promotion tolerated if you add value | Lead with insights, tips, and help. Mention your product only when directly relevant. |
| **Strict no-promotion** | Any product mention gets removed or flagged | Contribute genuinely. Build reputation. Let people discover your product through your profile. |

### Step 3: Design Your Contribution Strategy

Community marketing is 80% contribution, 20% promotion. Design a content mix:

**Value contributions (do these 4x for every promotional post):**
- Answer questions related to your domain expertise
- Share insights, frameworks, or lessons learned (from your own experience building)
- Comment thoughtfully on others' posts with substantive feedback
- Create resources or guides that help the community (checklists, templates, comparison tables)
- Share data, research, or case studies (even if small-scale)

**Build-in-public contributions:**
- Share your journey building the product (milestones, challenges, metrics)
- Ask for feedback on specific features or decisions
- Share behind-the-scenes of your process
- Celebrate wins and be transparent about failures

**Strategic product mentions (only when genuinely relevant):**
- When someone explicitly asks for a tool that does what yours does
- When sharing a solution to a problem your product solves (mention it naturally, not as a pitch)
- In designated self-promotion threads
- As part of a broader, value-rich post where the product is one piece of a larger point

### Step 4: Create a Content Calendar

Before creating the calendar, generate 5-10 specific topic categories based on the product domain. For an AI meeting notes tool, these would be: running remote meetings, note-taking systems, async work, client communication, productivity tool comparisons. These topics ground your contributions in genuine expertise rather than generic advice.

Plan your community engagement on a weekly cycle:

| Day | Community | Action | Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monday | [Primary community] | Answer 2-3 questions, comment on 3 posts | Value |
| Tuesday | [Secondary community] | Share a tip or insight related to your domain | Value |
| Wednesday | [Primary community] | Post build-in-public update or ask for feedback | Build-in-public |
| Thursday | [Tertiary community] | Engage with discussions, contribute expertise | Value |
| Friday | [Primary community] | Share a resource or guide (if applicable) | Value |

Time investment: 30-60 minutes per day. Consistency matters more than volume.

### Step 5: Track What Works

Measure community marketing effectiveness:

| Metric | How to Track |
|---|---|
| **Referral traffic** | UTM links or analytics showing visits from community platforms |
| **Sign-ups from community** | Ask "how did you find us?" or track referral source |
| **Karma / reputation** | Reddit karma, community rank, trust scores |
| **Engagement** | Comments, upvotes, saves on your posts |
| **Inbound DMs** | People reaching out because they saw your contributions |
| **Mentions by others** | Other community members recommending your product without being asked |

The last metric - unprompted mentions by others - is the holy grail of community marketing. When community members recommend your product voluntarily, you've built real trust.

## Common Mistakes

- **Promoting before contributing.** If your first post in a community is about your product, you've already lost. Contribute value for 2+ weeks before mentioning what you're building.
- **Treating communities as broadcast channels.** Communities are conversations, not billboards. Engage with others' content, don't just post yours.
- **Copy-pasting across communities.** Each community has its own culture. What works on Reddit won't work on LinkedIn. Adapt your message.
- **Giving up after a month.** Community marketing compounds. The first month feels like shouting into the void. By month 3, you're a recognised contributor with organic reach.
- **Ignoring the rules.** One banned post can permanently ruin your reputation in a community. Read the rules. Follow the rules.
- **Only engaging when you want something.** If you disappear between product launches, people notice. Show up consistently.

## Notes

- Community marketing is a compounding investment. Early returns are low, but 6-month returns are often the best of any channel.
- This skill pairs with channel-scorer (which tells you WHICH communities to focus on) and first-users-plan (which integrates community into your broader acquisition strategy).
- The communities you invest in now become your distribution channel later - for product updates, new features, content, and eventually new products.
