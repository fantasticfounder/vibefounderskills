# Build Your Email List Strategy

Social followers can vanish overnight when an algorithm changes. An email list is the one audience you actually own. This skill helps you design a lead magnet, set up capture points, write a welcome sequence, and build an email strategy that turns strangers into users and users into advocates.

## Context

You are helping a founder build an email list strategy for: **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides details about their product, audience, existing content, or marketing efforts, read those first. If a prior skill in this session produced audience data, growth loop analysis, or channel recommendations, use that context directly.

## Why This Matters

Three reasons email matters more than any other channel for indie founders:

1. **You own it.** Twitter can shadowban you. Reddit can remove your post. TikTok can kill your reach. Your email list is yours. No algorithm decides who sees your message.
2. **It converts better.** Email converts at 3-5x the rate of social media for most products. People who gave you their email address opted in - they're already warmer than a random social follower.
3. **It compounds.** Every subscriber stays on the list (until they unsubscribe). Unlike social posts that disappear in hours, your list grows over time and every email reaches the full audience.

### Step 1: Define the Email Strategy Foundation

Ask the user (if not already clear):
- What does your product do? Who is it for?
- Do you have an existing email list? How many subscribers?
- Do you have a blog, landing page, or any web presence?
- Are you active on any social platform? (Twitter, Reddit, YouTube, etc.)
- What's your goal for the email list? (drive product sign-ups, nurture leads, build audience for future products, all of the above)
- How much time can you spend on email per week? (be honest - 1 hour, 3 hours, 5 hours?)

### Step 2: Design the Lead Magnet

The lead magnet is the free thing that earns the email address. It must be valuable enough that someone trades their email for it, and relevant enough that it attracts your target users (not random freebie-seekers).

Evaluate these lead magnet types for the user's product:

**Checklist / Cheatsheet**
- What: A condensed, actionable version of a complex process
- Examples: "App Launch Day Checklist (47 Steps)", "SEO Audit Cheatsheet for Indie SaaS"
- Effort to create: Low (2-4 hours)
- Perceived value: Medium-high (saves time, reduces anxiety)
- Best when: Your audience faces a multi-step process they're nervous about

**Template / Swipe File**
- What: Something they can use immediately with minimal customisation
- Examples: "Cold Outreach Email Templates (5 Proven Formats)", "Pricing Page Copy Template"
- Effort to create: Low-medium (3-6 hours)
- Perceived value: High (immediately useful)
- Best when: Your audience needs to produce something they're not confident about

**Mini-Course (Email Sequence)**
- What: 5-7 emails over 1-2 weeks teaching one specific skill
- Examples: "5-Day Product Pricing Bootcamp", "7 Days to Your First 50 Users"
- Effort to create: Medium (8-15 hours for the full sequence)
- Perceived value: Very high (structured learning feels premium)
- Best when: Your audience wants to learn a skill related to your product

**Free Tool / Calculator**
- What: An interactive resource that delivers instant value
- Examples: "SaaS Pricing Calculator", "Should You Build This App? Quiz", "Market Size Estimator"
- Effort to create: Medium-high (depends on complexity)
- Perceived value: Very high (interactive, personalised)
- Best when: You can build something small that demonstrates your product's value

**Free Tier of Your Product**
- What: The product itself is the lead magnet, with email required for the free tier
- Examples: Any freemium product where sign-up requires email
- Effort to create: None (product already exists)
- Perceived value: Depends on the product
- Best when: Your product delivers clear value even in a limited free version

- **Relevance to product (1-5):** Does this attract your ACTUAL target users, or random people who want free stuff? 5 = directly related to the problem your product solves.

Recommend the highest-scoring option. If two tie, prefer lower effort (ship faster, learn faster).

### Step 3: Design Email Capture Points

Map every place you can capture an email address:

**Blog posts / Content pages**
- Content upgrade: a bonus resource specific to the article ("Download the full checklist from this post")
- Generic opt-in: sidebar or bottom-of-post email form
- Content upgrades convert 2-5x better than generic forms (assumption: warm traffic from relevant content)
- If the user doesn't have a blog: recommend starting with 3-5 posts targeting their audience's top questions

**Dedicated landing page**
- A standalone page with ONE purpose: capture the email
- No navigation, no distractions. Headline, sub-headline, benefit bullets, email form.
- Link from: social bios, community profiles, email signatures, guest post author bios
- Tools: Carrd ($19/year), ConvertKit landing pages (free), Ghost (free tier)

**Inside the product**
- In-app prompts for non-logged-in visitors
- Onboarding email capture (if your product allows anonymous use)
- Feature gates: "Get weekly usage reports by email"
- Post-value moments: "Want tips on getting more from [product]? We send one email per week."

**Social bio links**
- Every social profile should link to either the landing page or the lead magnet
- Use a link-in-bio tool if you need multiple links (Linktree, Bento, custom page)

**Community contributions**
- When you answer questions on Reddit, forums, or Discord, link to a relevant resource (not your product directly)
- "I wrote a detailed guide on this: [link to blog post with email capture]"
- This builds authority AND captures emails from people who already trust your advice

### Step 4: Design the Welcome Sequence (5 Emails)

The welcome sequence is the most important email you'll write. It runs automatically for every new subscriber and sets the tone for your entire relationship. Here's the structure:

**Email 1 - Day 0: Deliver + Introduce**
- Subject line: "[Lead Magnet Name] is here" or "Your [resource] is ready"
- Deliver the lead magnet immediately (link or attachment)
- Introduce yourself in 2-3 sentences. Who are you? Why should they listen to you?
- Set expectations: what kind of emails will they get, how often
- One personal touch: "Hit reply and tell me what you're working on. I read every response."

**Email 2 - Day 2: Quick Win**
- Subject line: Focus on an immediate result they can get
- Teach ONE actionable thing related to the lead magnet topic
- This should take them 15 minutes or less to implement
- Purpose: prove that your emails deliver value. If they open email 2 and get a win, they'll open email 3.

**Email 3 - Day 4: Teach a Framework**
- Subject line: Name the framework or concept
- Go deeper. Teach a real framework, mental model, or strategy.
- This is where you demonstrate expertise. Don't hold back - give them something they'd pay for.
- Include an example or case study to make it concrete.

**Email 4 - Day 7: Story or Case Study**
- Subject line: Focus on the transformation or result
- Tell a story: your own journey, a user's success, or a well-known example in their industry.
- Stories build trust and make abstract strategies feel real.
- End with a takeaway they can apply to their own situation.

**Email 5 - Day 10: Soft Pitch**
- Subject line: Address their core problem directly
- Recap the value you've delivered in the first 4 emails.
- Introduce your product as the natural next step for someone who wants to go further.
- No hard sell. Frame it as: "If you've found these emails useful, [product] takes it further by [specific benefit]."
- Include a clear call to action (link to product, free trial, demo).

**Key principles for the full sequence:**
- Write like a person, not a brand. First person. Conversational.
- Every email should deliver standalone value (even if they never open another one)
- Keep emails under 500 words. Respect their time.
- Use plain text or minimal formatting. Fancy HTML templates often perform worse for indie products.

### Step 5: Design the Ongoing Strategy

After the welcome sequence, you need a regular cadence. Here's how to sustain it:

**Frequency**: Weekly or biweekly. Pick one and be consistent. Weekly is better for audience building, biweekly is more sustainable for solo founders. Never email less than monthly (people forget who you are).

**Content types to rotate:**
- Tactical how-to (teach one specific thing)
- Framework or mental model (explain a concept they can apply)
- Behind the scenes (what you're building, why, what you learned)
- Curated resources (3-5 links you found useful, with your commentary)
- User spotlight (feature a user's success with your product)
- Product update (new feature, but framed as the user benefit, not the feature)

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't email only when you want something (product launch, sale). Build trust first.
- Don't write corporate-sounding emails. You're a person, not a marketing department.
- Don't buy email lists. Ever. The addresses are bad, the engagement is zero, and it can get your domain blacklisted.

**Metrics to track:**
- Open rate: 30-50% is healthy for a small list. Under 20% means your subject lines need work or your audience isn't engaged.
- Click rate: 2-5% is normal. Over 5% is excellent.
- Unsubscribe rate: Under 0.5% per email is healthy. Over 1% means you're sending too often or the content isn't matching expectations.
- Reply rate: The most underrated metric. Replies mean engagement, trust, and feedback. Optimise for replies.

### Step 6: Tool Comparison for Solo Founders

| Tool | Free Tier | Best For | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ghost** | Free (self-hosted) or $9/mo | Newsletter + membership site | Beautiful, owns your content, built-in membership/payments | Self-hosting requires technical setup |
| **ConvertKit** | Free up to 10K subs | Creators building automated sequences | Visual automations, tagging, landing pages | Free tier limits features |
| **Buttondown** | Free up to 100 subs | Minimalist newsletter | Simple, developer-friendly, Markdown support | Fewer automation features |
| **Substack** | Free | Writers who want built-in audience | Zero setup, built-in discovery | You don't fully own the platform, limited customisation |
| **Resend + React Email** | Free up to 3K/mo | Developers who want full control | Full customisation, API-first | Requires coding, no built-in subscriber management |

**Recommendation logic:**
- If you want simplicity and don't code: ConvertKit or Substack
- If you want to own everything and can self-host: Ghost
- If you're a developer who wants full control: Buttondown or Resend
- If you just want to start TODAY with zero friction: Substack (migrate later if needed)

## Common Mistakes

- **No lead magnet.** "Subscribe to my newsletter" is not a value proposition. People need a reason to give you their email.
- **Lead magnet attracts the wrong audience.** A "Free iPhone Giveaway" will get emails, but those people don't care about your product. The lead magnet must be relevant to what you sell.
- **Welcome sequence is all pitch, no value.** If every email says "buy my product," people unsubscribe. Deliver real value in emails 1-4 so the pitch in email 5 feels earned.
- **Inconsistent sending.** Emailing once, then disappearing for 3 months, then blasting 5 emails = people forget you and unsubscribe. Pick a cadence and stick to it.
- **Fancy HTML templates.** For indie products, plain text emails from a real person outperform corporate-looking HTML templates. Your audience wants a human, not a brand.

## Notes

- Starting from zero is fine. Every email list started at zero. Your first 100 subscribers are the hardest and most valuable.
- Quality over quantity. 500 engaged subscribers who open every email and reply regularly are worth more than 10,000 dead addresses.
- Email and product are symbiotic. Your emails drive people to the product. Your product drives people to the email list. Design both sides.
- If you haven't defined your growth loops yet, run the growth-loops skill first. Email is one channel in a broader growth system.
